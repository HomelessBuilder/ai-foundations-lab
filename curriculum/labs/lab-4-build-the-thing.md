# Module 4 — Building Something Small
## Lab 4: "Build the Thing"

*Status: Draft — for Diego's review.*

**What you'll walk away with:** A real, working micro-project — however
small — built inside your project folder with Claude Code's help.

**Time:** This lab is intentionally open-ended — it might take 60
minutes, or it might take longer. If you need to stop and come back,
that's completely fine. Lab 5 picks up right where you leave off.

---

### Before We Start

Everything up to now has been setup: the tool, the folder, the context,
the journal. All of it exists to make this lab possible. Today we
actually build something.

Here's the thing to hold onto going in: **you will not get this exactly
right on the first try, and that's not a problem — that's how this
works.** Building with AI is a back-and-forth, not a vending machine.
We're going to practice that back-and-forth on purpose today.

---

### Step 1 — Pick Something Small

If you already have an idea of something small you'd like to build, use
that. If you're drawing a blank, here are a few starting points — pick
whichever sounds most like you:

- **A personal webpage** — one file, your name, a bit about you, maybe a
  favorite hobby or photo. Something you could open and look at.
- **A cheat sheet** — take something you already know well (a recipe, a
  hobby, a routine) and have Claude Code help you turn it into a clean,
  organized reference file.
- **A small game** — something simple and text-based, like a number
  guessing game or a Mad Libs generator.
- **A formatted to-do list or FAQ document** — take something you
  already need to organize and have Claude Code help turn it into a
  clean, structured file.

None of these need to be impressive. Small and working beats big and
half-finished, every time.

One heads-up: in the examples through the rest of this lab, and again in
Lab 5, we'll often use "a webpage" to illustrate a point — that's just
because it's easy to describe in one sentence, not a hint that it's the
best choice. Whatever you actually picked, keep swapping it in mentally
wherever you see it.

---

### Step 2 — Ask for It — Vaguely First, Then Better

Let's actually feel the difference iteration makes. Start by asking
Claude Code for your project in the vaguest way you can — something
like:

> "Can you make me a webpage?"

See what you get. It'll probably be reasonable, but generic — it doesn't
know anything about *you* yet, because you didn't tell it anything.

Now try again, this time with real detail:

> "Can you make me a single webpage with my name as the title, two
> sentences about who I am, and a short list of things I'm interested
> in?"

> **Worth knowing:** this is the difference between a vague ask and a
> well-scoped one. Both are valid ways to start a conversation — but the
> second one gets you something closer to what you actually wanted, in
> fewer rounds. Neither one is "the wrong way to prompt." Vague is often
> where you *start* when you're still figuring out what you want.
> Specific is where you go once you know.

---

### Step 3 — Build It, Then Push Back

Once you've got a first version, actually look at it. Open the file, run
it, read it — whatever "check it out" means for what you built. Then
tell Claude Code what to change. Don't just accept the first draft if
something's off.

> If you built a webpage, find the `.html` file in your folder and
> double-click it — your browser will open it. A webpage is meant to be
> viewed, not read as plain text.

Try things like:
- "Can you make this shorter?"
- "I don't like this part — can we try something different here?"
- "This isn't quite what I meant — I actually wanted..."

> **This part trips people up, and it shouldn't.** If your first version
> isn't right, that doesn't mean you asked badly or that something went
> wrong. Course-correcting *is* the process — nobody building real things
> with AI gets it exact on the first pass. The people who seem fast at
> this aren't getting it right first try. They're just fast at noticing
> what's off and saying so clearly.

Keep going until you have something you're actually satisfied with —
even if "satisfied" just means "this is small, but it's real, and it's
mine."

---

### Step 4 — Sum It Up

Before we close out, ask Claude Code:

```
Can you give me a quick summary of what we built and what files are in my project folder?
```

That gives you a clean picture of what you made, in one place, before we
move on — and it's exactly what you'll hand to your journal in Lab 5.

---

### Where We Land

You just built something — really built it, through a back-and-forth
that got better each round instead of trying to nail it in one shot.
That skill matters more than the project itself. Every real project from
here on is going to use exactly this loop: ask, look, adjust, repeat.

We've got this. Next lab, we log what just happened in your journal —
and set up something you're going to want to see for yourself.
